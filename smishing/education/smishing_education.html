Smishing attacks arrive as text messages (SMS) — not just email. They use the same social engineering techniques but are often harder to spot on a small screen.

Why Smishing Is Growing Fast

98%
SMS open rate — compared to ~20% for email. Attackers know your texts get read.
3×
Higher click rate on mobile phishing links vs desktop, due to smaller screens hiding URLs.
1 in 3
US adults report receiving a smishing text in any given month (2023 Proofpoint data).

Most Common Smishing Scenarios

  • Package delivery: "Your USPS/FedEx/UPS package couldn't be delivered — reschedule here" (especially effective since everyone orders online)
  • Bank fraud alerts: "Suspicious transaction blocked — verify now" using fear of financial loss
  • Government impersonation: IRS, Social Security, DMV, Customs — unpaid fees, refunds, or arrest warrants
  • Prize/lottery scams: "You've won — click to claim before it expires"
  • Corporate IT: "Your VPN certificate is expiring — renew via this link"
  • Two-factor code requests: "We need to verify your identity — click here to confirm your 2FA code"

How to Spot Smishing

  • Unexpected messages: Real delivery companies only text if you specifically signed up for alerts
  • Shortened or suspicious URLs: bit.ly/delivery or usps-track.net instead of usps.com
  • Urgency + financial threat: "Act now or your account is suspended / package returned / fee charged"
  • Unknown sender numbers: Often overseas numbers or short codes not associated with the real company
  • Grammar and formatting: Unusual capitalization, awkward phrasing, generic greetings
  • Requests for credentials or payment: Legitimate services handle these in-app or on their verified website

How to Stay Safe from Smishing

  1. Never click links in unexpected texts — go directly to the company's website or app to check status
  2. Verify via official channels: For package alerts, use the tracking number on the carrier's official app/website
  3. Don't reply or call back unknown numbers — this confirms your number is active
  4. Report smishing to your carrier: Forward suspicious texts to 7726 (SPAM) — works on all major US carriers
  5. Report to IT Security if you receive a work-related smishing text
  6. Use your phone's built-in spam filtering (iOS/Android both have SMS filtering features)
