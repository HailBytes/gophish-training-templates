Attackers impersonate DocuSign, Adobe Sign, and similar platforms to steal credentials or get victims to sign fraudulent documents.

Why E-Signature Attacks Are Effective

E-signature platforms are used for legally binding agreements — NDAs, contracts, HR documents. Attackers exploit these because:

  • High-stakes feeling: "Legal document requiring signature" creates urgency and importance
  • Trusted platforms: DocuSign and Adobe Sign are used by nearly every business
  • Expiration pressure: "Document expires in 48 hours" prevents careful evaluation
  • Credential theft: Fake signing pages steal your SSO/Microsoft/Google credentials
  • Malware delivery: Some attacks deliver malicious "document" files instead of real PDFs

Common Attack Patterns

  • Fake NDA/contract signing: Convincing you to enter credentials to access a document that doesn't exist
  • HR document spoofing: Impersonating HR for policy changes, offer letters, or amendments
  • Vendor impersonation: Fake documents appearing to come from partners or clients
  • Credential-harvesting signing pages: The "signing portal" is a fake login page
  • Malicious PDF payloads: The downloaded "document" executes malware

How to Spot Fake E-Signature Emails

  • Sender domain: Real DocuSign emails come from @docusign.net, Adobe Sign from @adobesign.com — verify before clicking
  • Unexpected documents: If you weren't told to expect a document to sign, verify with the sender via a separate channel (call, not reply)
  • Tight deadlines: "Sign in 48 hours or it expires" is pressure to act without thinking
  • Login required before viewing: DocuSign shows the document before asking for credentials
  • Generic sender: Real signing requests identify the specific person or organization sending the document

Safe E-Signature Habits

  1. Verify with the sender before signing anything — call or message them through a separate channel
  2. Check the sender domain carefully: docusign.net vs docu-sign.net
  3. Log in directly to DocuSign/Adobe Sign by navigating to the known URL, not via the email link
  4. Real signing links don't ask for your Microsoft/Google password — signing is done via the platform's own authentication
  5. When in doubt, ask your IT or Legal team to verify a document request before you sign
