Quishing (QR Code Phishing) — Training

You scanned or clicked a simulated malicious QR code.
"Quishing" uses QR codes to bypass email security filters and redirect victims to phishing pages — a rapidly growing attack vector since 2023.

Why QR Codes Are Weaponized

QR codes defeat traditional email security because:
  • Security scanners can't read image URLs — the malicious URL is hidden inside a picture, bypassing link-scanning filters
  • Scan happens on your phone — mobile devices often have fewer security controls than corporate laptops
  • People trust QR codes — pandemic-era QR menu adoption normalized scanning unknown codes
  • No visible link to hover over — you can't see where the QR code leads before scanning

Quishing attacks increased over 500% in 2023 as attackers adapted to bypass email security gateways that now scan links but not images.

Common Quishing Attack Scenarios

  • MFA re-enrollment: "Security upgrade requires you to re-register your authenticator" — QR leads to credential harvest page
  • WiFi credential provisioning: "Scan to connect to new secure network" — QR leads to fake portal
  • Parking payment: Physical QR code stickers placed on parking meters (in-person quishing)
  • Package tracking: "Scan to confirm delivery address" — extremely effective
  • Microsoft/Office 365 MFA: Fake "scan to set up Microsoft Authenticator" links in HR onboarding emails
  • Conference/event check-in: Fake event QR codes that capture registration credentials

Red Flags Before Scanning

  • Unexpected MFA/security re-enrollment emails — IT announces these in advance through multiple channels
  • Urgency + QR code combination: "Scan immediately or lose access" should always raise suspicion
  • Physical QR codes in unusual places: Stickers placed over original QR codes at restaurants, meters, etc.
  • No company branding on the QR itself: Legitimate IT tools embed QR codes with identifiable context
  • The email mentions a "manual link" as backup: This is the real attack vector — the link and the QR both lead to the same phishing page

Safe QR Code Practices

  1. Preview the URL before opening: iOS and Android both show the destination URL when you scan — read it before tapping "Open"
  2. Verify IT announcements through official channels — check the intranet or call the help desk before scanning a QR for "mandatory re-enrollment"
  3. Don't scan QR codes in unsolicited emails — especially those about MFA, credentials, or account security
  4. Inspect physical QR codes for stickers placed over original codes (common on parking meters, menus)
  5. Use a QR scanner with built-in URL safety checks — many security apps offer this
  6. Report suspicious QR code emails to IT Security — they're particularly hard to catch with automated filters
