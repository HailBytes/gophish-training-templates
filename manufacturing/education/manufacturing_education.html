Supply chain attacks target the trusted relationships between manufacturers, suppliers, and vendors — compromising one can cascade to many.

🏭 Why Manufacturing Is a High-Value Target

Manufacturing and supply chain phishing targets the interconnected relationships between companies. Attackers aim to:

  • Steal intellectual property (blueprints, formulas, manufacturing processes)
  • Commit invoice fraud by redirecting payments to attacker accounts
  • Access ERP systems (SAP, Oracle) containing sensitive business data
  • Compromise OT/ICS credentials for potential sabotage
  • Use supplier access to pivot into customer networks (supply chain attacks)

🎭 Common Supply Chain Attack Patterns

  • Supplier portal credential theft: Fake "update required" emails for supplier portals (Ariba, Coupa, etc.)
  • Invoice fraud (BEC): Compromised supplier email used to redirect payment to attacker accounts
  • RFQ/bid document delivery: Malicious attachments in quote requests
  • Shipping/logistics notifications: Fake tracking updates with malware links
  • ERP access re-verification: "Your SAP/Oracle access requires renewal" credential theft

🚩 Red Flags

  • Unexpected portal access requests: Contact IT or your supplier directly to verify
  • Banking change requests from suppliers: Always verify by phone using a number from your records, not the email
  • Urgency around payments or orders: "Confirm by EOD or the order is cancelled"
  • Unfamiliar supplier domains: Attackers register lookalike domains (supplier-corp.com vs suppliercorp.com)
  • Attachments in RFQ responses: Verify the supplier sent it before opening

✅ Protecting the Supply Chain

  1. Verify banking changes by phone — call your supplier contact using your existing records before changing payment details
  2. Access portals directly — use bookmarked URLs for supplier portals, not email links
  3. Implement payment verification procedures for any changes to payment information
  4. Be suspicious of urgency around payments, access, or document deadlines
  5. Report suspicious supplier communications to both IT Security and your procurement team

🧠 Knowledge Check

You receive an email that appears to be from a regular supplier saying they've changed banks and asking you to update their payment details for upcoming invoices. What do you do?

Correct Answer: B — Business Email Compromise (BEC) targeting payment changes is one of the costliest cybercrime categories. The attacker may have compromised your supplier's email — always call using your existing contact number, never the one in the email.
