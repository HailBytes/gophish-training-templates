💬 Why Attackers Target Slack, Teams, and Zoom

Collaboration platforms are among the most trusted tools in the modern workplace. Attackers exploit this trust by sending notifications that look identical to real platform emails. These attacks are particularly dangerous because:

  • High trust: Employees expect and rely on these notifications daily
  • Volume: Heavy users receive dozens of real notifications, making fake ones easy to miss
  • Urgency: "Your account will be suspended" or "approval required by 5pm" triggers panic
  • Workplace authority: Messages appear to come from managers or admins

🎭 Platform-Specific Attack Patterns

Slack Attacks

  • Fake "account audit" requiring credential re-verification
  • Forged DM previews appearing to be from your admin
  • Workspace suspension warnings with urgent login links
  • Fake app authorization requests

Microsoft Teams Attacks

  • Fabricated guest access approval requests with auto-approve deadlines
  • Fake meeting invitations with malicious join links
  • Impersonated IT admin messages requesting credential confirmation
  • Spoofed file-sharing notifications

Zoom Attacks

  • Fake recording-ready notifications (especially for "sensitive" meetings)
  • Account storage-full warnings forcing immediate download
  • Fake meeting invitations with malicious links
  • Credential theft via fake sign-in pages

🚩 Red Flags to Watch For

  • Sender domain: Real Slack emails come from @slack.com, Teams from @microsoft.com, Zoom from @zoom.us — hover before clicking
  • Login-via-email links: These platforms never require you to re-enter credentials via an email link
  • Auto-approve threats: "Your access will be granted automatically if you don't respond" is a pressure tactic
  • Mismatched urgency: Real platform notifications aren't urgent unless you've set up actual alerts
  • Generic recordings: You would know if you hosted a meeting — check your app directly

✅ Safe Habits for Collaboration Platforms

  1. Go to the app directly — never sign in via an email link; open the app or navigate to the known URL
  2. Hover over sender addresses — verify the domain before clicking anything
  3. Treat urgency as a red flag — real approval workflows don't auto-approve without you
  4. Enable login notifications in your Slack/Teams/Zoom settings so you know about real sign-ins
  5. Use SSO/MFA — even if credentials are stolen, MFA prevents account takeover
  6. Report suspicious emails to IT before clicking — even if they look real
