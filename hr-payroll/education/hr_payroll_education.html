💸 The Direct Financial Impact

HR and payroll phishing is uniquely dangerous because a successful attack can result in your actual paycheck being stolen. The FBI reports thousands of payroll diversion cases annually, with average losses exceeding $10,000 per victim. These attacks involve:

  • Redirecting direct deposit to attacker-controlled bank accounts
  • Stealing benefits enrollment credentials to submit fraudulent claims
  • Accessing W-2 and tax documents for identity theft
  • Submitting fraudulent expense reports or PTO changes

🎭 Common HR Phishing Patterns

  • Direct deposit change requests: "Update your banking information before the payroll deadline"
  • Benefits open enrollment: Timed to real enrollment periods to maximize believability
  • W-2/tax document access: "Your tax documents are ready — log in to download"
  • Policy update acknowledgment: "You must acknowledge the new HR policy by Friday"
  • Performance review access: Links to "review your performance evaluation" that steal credentials
  • CEO/executive impersonation: Fake emails from leadership asking HR to process payroll changes

🚩 Red Flags in HR Emails

  • Unexpected payroll or banking update requests: Changes to direct deposit are initiated by you in the HR portal, not by clicking an email link
  • Enrollment outside normal periods: Benefits enrollment has specific, announced windows — unexpected "emergency enrollment" is a red flag
  • Generic HR sender: Your real HR team has names you recognize; look up the sender in the directory
  • Tight deadlines on financial changes: "Update by 5 PM or miss this pay cycle" creates panic
  • Links to external portals: Your HR platform has a known, consistent URL — unexpected domains are suspicious

✅ Safe Payroll & HR Practices

  1. Make payroll changes only by logging in directly to your HR system using your bookmarked URL
  2. Call HR directly using the number from your employee directory if you receive any banking or payroll change requests
  3. Never update banking information via an email link — this is the #1 payroll diversion attack vector
  4. Verify W-2/tax documents by logging into your HR platform's tax documents section directly
  5. Report suspicious HR emails to IT Security immediately — payroll attacks are often targeted and timed to pay periods
