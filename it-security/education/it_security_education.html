About IT Infrastructure Phishing

You received a simulated phishing email that impersonated your IT department. These attacks are particularly dangerous because:

  • Authority: People trust IT department communications
  • Technical Language: IT jargon makes emails seem legitimate
  • Urgency: "System issues need immediate attention"
  • Access: IT emails often request credential verification

Common IT Phishing Techniques

Techniques Used Against You:

  • Server Migration Story: "We had technical issues that affected your account"
  • Time Pressure: "Verify your account immediately"
  • Security Theater: "Click here to secure your account"
  • Personal Touch: Using your actual email address to seem legitimate
  • Internal Authority: Claiming to be from your IT department

Red Flags You Should Have Noticed

Warning Signs in IT Phishing Emails:

  • Unexpected IT Communications: Real IT usually announces maintenance in advance
  • Generic Greetings: IT knows your name and employee ID
  • External Links: Real IT uses internal systems or known portals
  • Credential Requests: IT never asks for passwords via email
  • Urgency Without Context: Real issues come with tickets and proper communication
  • Poor Formatting: Official IT communications follow company templates

How to Verify Legitimate IT Communications

Before Acting on IT Emails:

  1. Check Internal Announcements: Look at company intranet/announcements
  2. Contact IT Directly: Call or visit IT support using known contact methods
  3. Use Official Channels: Log into company systems through bookmarked URLs
  4. Verify Sender: Real IT emails come from your company domain
  5. Look for Tickets: Legitimate IT work usually has ticket numbers
  6. Ask Colleagues: Others would receive similar legitimate notices

What Attackers Were After

This attack aimed to steal:
  • Your network login credentials
  • Access to company systems and data
  • Personal information for identity theft
  • A foothold to launch further attacks

Remember: One compromised account can lead to a company-wide breach!

Knowledge Check: IT Security

Scenario Question:

You receive an email from "IT Support" saying your password will expire in 24 hours and you need to click a link to update it. What do you do?




Click for Answer & Explanation

Correct Answer: C - Always verify IT requests through official channels. Real IT departments have established procedures for password changes and will never ask you to click email links for security updates.

Best Practices for IT Security

Golden Rules:

  • Never provide credentials via email - IT will never ask for passwords
  • Use official company portals - Bookmark legitimate IT service pages
  • Verify unusual requests - Call IT if something seems off
  • Keep software updated - Real updates come through official channels
  • Report suspicious emails - Help protect your colleagues
