What just happened?

You received an email appearing to be from Microsoft Copilot claiming your license was suspended. In a real attack, clicking the link would have sent you to a fake Microsoft login page designed to steal your credentials — giving attackers full access to your Microsoft 365 account, email, files, and Teams conversations.

Why AI-tool phishing is so effective

AI tools like Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT, and GitHub Copilot are now deeply embedded in daily work. Employees depend on them and fear losing access — especially mid-task. Attackers exploit this urgency. A "license suspended" notification creates immediate panic, causing people to click before they think.

These emails are increasingly convincing because AI is used to draft them, because attackers know the exact product names and workflows, and because many employees are still unfamiliar with what legitimate notifications from these services look like.

Red flags in this email

  • Urgency and threat of loss "Your license will be permanently revoked within 48 hours" is a pressure tactic. Legitimate services give far longer notice and go through official IT channels.
  • Suspicious sender address Legitimate Microsoft emails come from @microsoft.com. Always hover over the sender name to reveal the actual email address before acting.
  • Link destination doesn't match the brand Hovering over the button before clicking would show a URL that does not end in microsoft.com. Phishers use lookalike domains like micros0ft-auth.com or login-microsoft365.net.
  • License issues are handled by your IT team Microsoft never asks end users to directly re-verify a Copilot license. These actions are performed by your IT administrator in the Microsoft Admin Center.
  • Request for credentials via email link Microsoft will never ask you to re-enter your password by clicking an email link. If prompted to sign in, navigate directly to portal.office.com instead.

What to do when you receive a suspicious AI-tool email

  • Don't click — go directly to the source

    If you're concerned about your Copilot or ChatGPT subscription, open a new browser tab and navigate directly to the service's website. Never click links in suspicious emails.

  • Verify with your IT help desk

    Contact your IT team directly (not via a number in the email) to confirm whether any license action is genuine. They can check your account status in minutes.

  • Report the email to security

    Forward suspicious emails to your security team at security@company.com or use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook. Every report helps protect your colleagues.

  • Enable MFA on all AI tool accounts

    Multi-factor authentication means stolen credentials alone cannot grant an attacker access. Ensure MFA is enabled on your Microsoft 365, ChatGPT, and GitHub accounts.

  • Hover before you click

    Always hover over any link to preview its destination. If the URL doesn't exactly match the brand's official domain, treat the email as phishing.
